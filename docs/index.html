Multi-objective optimization (MOO) is a field of particle swarm optimization (PSO) where 2 or 3 objectives need to be optimised simultaneously. These objectives often have conflicting goals and are therefore difficult to solve. This paper investigates a novel approach to solving MOO problems by combining aspects of the standard multi-guide PSO (MGPSO) developed by Scheepers, Engelbrecht and Cleghorn [2] with the multi-modal speciation PSO developed by Xiaodong [6]. The novel algorithm introduced, referred to throughout the paper as the speciation MGPSO algorithm, groups particles in each swarm into species whilst utilising the archive guide to share information between subswarms as is seen in the standard MGPSO algorithm. The two algorithms are compared using three performance measures: inverted generational distance (IGD), hypervolume (HV) and visual plots comparing the approximated pareto optimal front (POF) generated by each algorithm per benchmark problem with the true POF for the respective problem. In all performance measures the standard MGPSO algorithm outperforms the speciation MGPSO algorithm, reiterating the importance of not diverting to exploiting local regions too early when solving MOO problems.

Meta-heuristics for optimizing functions have been around for many years. In this paper, two examples of meta-heuristics from the 1960s, pattern search and the Nelder Mead direct search algorithms, are compared with meta-heuristics developed in the mid to late 1990s, differential evolution and inertia weight particle swarm optimization (PSO), to determine whether approaches to optimization have improved since the 1960s. The latter two are the source of many modern optimization methods that are still commonly used both in research and in industry. The algorithms are compared against each other and a purely stochastic PSO. The results show that the pattern search and Nelder Mead algorithms outperform the standard differential evolution and inertia weight PSO algorithms across the majority of the chosen benchmark problems, occasionally outperformed by the inertia weight PSO. These results indicate that the chosen approaches from the 1960s are still relevant and that elements of these approaches to optimization should still be considered when developing novel methods.

Recurrent neural networks (RNNs) are a class of neural networks commonly applied to modelling time series datasets. In this paper, three varieties of RNNs, the Elman, Jordan and multi RNNs, are compared across five datasets, three of which are non-stationary and two stationary. In order to compare the performance of the three RNNs, their average loss and mean absolute percentage error across the five datasets are compared. On average, the multi RNN, a combination of the Elman and Jordan RNNs, performs best. Additional remarks are given for datasets where the Elman or Jordan RNN outperforms the multi-RNN and importance is placed on designing applicable network architectures based on the datasets they will be applied to.

Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) is a field of Artificial Intelligence utilising particles acting as a swarm to solve optimization problems. Efficient solving of these problems is necessary in a diverse array of domains. This paper investigates four different hybrid implementations of standard PSO algorithms and the sine-cosine algorithm first described by Mirjalili [6]. The four hybrid algorithms differ in how particle behaviours are assigned and change throughout the optimization process. The hybrid algorithms are compared to standard star topology and ring topology inertia weight PSOs and the sine-cosine algorithm in a basic statistical analysis and graphs are plotted for fitness, diversity and boundary violations across five 30-dimensional benchmark problems. Three of the four hybrid algorithms show significant improvement in performance over the standard PSO and sine-cosine algorithms. The results demonstrate the utility of heterogeneous PSO algorithms combining standard PSO practices with the sine-cosine algorithm as well as highlight the importance of evaluating emergent behaviour when combining PSO techniques.

This paper presents an exploratory data analysis (EDA) into a dataset containing 31 features relating to breast cancer tumours and their successful diagnoses. The EDA was conducted using a Kohonen self-organizing feature map (SOM) with the goal of discovering which input features have the closest relationship with the true diagnosis of a breast cancer tumour. A brief background on SOMs with respect to the aim of this paper is given before a description of the pre-processing performed to shape the dataset into a suitable form for training a SOM. The trained SOM and its weights per input feature are used to determine the most important features indicating whether a tumour is malignant or benign. Some of the features present in the dataset are found to have little to no effect on the true diagnosis, highlighting the utility SOMs provide in the world of data science and data analytics.

Ensemble learning is an approach in machine learning where the power of multiple machine learning models are combined to produce a single model that reaps the benefits of all its base models or base learners. This paper presents a comparison of heterogeneous and homogeneous ensemble methods. Four models are compared, where two are heterogeneous and two are homogeneous, in an attempt to understand which of the two approaches results in better classification performance. The models are compared using a breast cancer dataset where tumour descriptors classify whether a tumour is malignant or benign. Of the four models, the two heterogeneous ensemble models perform best highlighting the effect that combining different base models has on the overall performance of the ensemble model.

The English Premier League (EPL) is one of the oldest and most popular professional sporting leagues in the world. Due to its popularity, an estimated 2.7 million pounds is bet per EPL match [1] in the United Kingdom alone, with some websites claiming up to 1 billion euros are bet on each EPL match worldwide. With these figures only predicted to rise in the future, a great deal of data science techniques have been applied to EPL datasets, hoping to uncover patterns that lead to better betting decisions and increased profit. This paper aims to explore questions a casual, non-betting fan may want answered. Questions such as, which half is more exciting, the second or first half?, or which teams can I expect to battle it out for the championship trophy year after year and which teams are in a constant struggle in the middle of the pack?
